Geometry in Church Architecture

Quarter 1 Project-Based Learning Investigation

FLEx Phase: Express

Learning Targets

  • Analyze and identify parallel and perpendicular lines in architectural structures
  • Apply angle relationships (complementary, supplementary, vertical, corresponding) to real-world design
  • Explore how geometric principles reflect God's order and human creativity as image-bearers
  • Design an architectural element using precise geometric constructions
  • Communicate mathematical reasoning through research, analysis, and visual presentation

Instructions

Over the next 9 weeks, you will investigate how geometry shapes sacred spaces. Your project has four main components:

1. Research (Weeks 1-3): Select a historic church building and research its architectural features. Identify examples of parallel lines, perpendicular lines, and angle relationships in the structure. Document your findings with images, measurements (or estimates), and descriptions.

2. Data & Analysis (Weeks 4-5): Create detailed geometric analysis of at least three architectural features. Measure or calculate angles, identify line relationships, and explain how these geometric principles contribute to the building's structure, beauty and theological story.

3. Design (Weeks 6-7): Design ONE architectural element inspired by your research—such as a rose window, floor plan, facade, or doorway. Your design must emphasize lines rather than curves and demonstrate mastery of parallel/perpendicular relationships and angle properties. Include measurements and geometric justifications.

4. Peer Review & Revision (Week 8): Exchange projects with two classmates. Provide constructive feedback using the peer review template. Revise your work based on feedback received.

Groups: You may work individually or in pairs. If working in a pair, clearly identify which components each person completed.

Research Requirements

Your research should address:

  • Historical Context: When and where was the church built? What architectural style does it represent (Gothic, Romanesque, Byzantine, Modern, etc.)?
  • Geometric Features: Where do you observe parallel lines? Perpendicular lines? What types of angles appear in the design (right angles, acute, obtuse)?
  • Structural Purpose: How do these geometric relationships contribute to the building's stability and function?
  • Theological Symbolism: How might the geometric precision reflect Biblical themes of order, beauty, or God's character?

Use at least 3 credible sources (books, architectural databases, museum websites, academic articles). Avoid unreliable sources like Wikipedia as a primary reference.

Submission Format

Submit your final project as a slideshow, PDF, or document that includes:

  • Title page with your name(s) and church selected
  • Research section with sources cited
  • Data/analysis section with labeled diagrams and geometric explanations
  • Design section with your original architectural element (drawn digitally or by hand, then scanned)
  • Peer review section (completed template)
  • Reflection: 1-2 paragraphs on how this project deepened your understanding of geometry and God's creative order

Vocabulary

  • Parallel lines: Lines in the same plane that never intersect
  • Perpendicular lines: Lines that intersect at a 90° angle
  • Transversal: A line that intersects two or more lines
  • Corresponding angles: Angles in the same relative position when a transversal crosses parallel lines
  • Alternate interior angles: Non-adjacent angles on opposite sides of a transversal, between parallel lines
  • Complementary angles: Two angles whose measures sum to 90°
  • Supplementary angles: Two angles whose measures sum to 180°
  • Vertical angles: Opposite angles formed by two intersecting lines (always congruent)
  • Facade: The front or face of a building
  • Nave: The central part of a church building

General Background

Throughout history, architects have used geometric principles to create buildings that inspire awe and reflect divine order. Church architecture, in particular, demonstrates how mathematics and faith intersect. From the soaring perpendicular lines of Gothic cathedrals to the precise symmetry of Byzantine domes, geometric relationships create both structural integrity and spiritual symbolism.

Parallel lines in columns create rhythm and unity. Perpendicular intersections form crosses, symbolizing Christ's sacrifice. Angle relationships in vaulted ceilings distribute weight while drawing the eye heavenward. These are not accidents—they reflect intentional design rooted in mathematical truth.

As image-bearers of God, humans possess creativity and the capacity for order. When we study geometry in architecture, we see both God's mathematical laws (which govern structure and stability) and human ingenuity (which applies those laws to create beauty). This project invites you to explore that intersection: How does geometry reveal God's character? How does our creative work reflect His image in us?

Kingdom Domain Integration

COG (Cognitive): Understanding truth through Scripture

Focus: Students will explore how geometric order in creation reflects God's nature as a God of order, not chaos (1 Corinthians 14:33). Just as parallel lines maintain consistent relationships and perpendicular lines create perfect right angles, God's truth is consistent, precise, and unchanging. The mathematical laws governing architecture point to the Logos (Word) through whom all things were made (John 1:3)—the same rational principle that makes mathematics possible and reliable.
