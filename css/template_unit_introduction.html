What is Geometry?

Geometry isn't just about memorizing definitions of shapes. It's about discovering how shapes emerge from operations—from the actions we perform with tools like compass, straightedge, and rotation.

In the beginning, God spoke creation into existence through action: "And God said... and it was so." (Genesis 1) The operation came first; the creation emerged from it.

In this course, you'll discover geometry the same way: by operating—constructing, transforming, measuring—and watching shapes emerge from your actions.

Materials: You will need: (1) notebook with grid paper, (2) adjustable compass, (3) ruler/straight edge, (4) protractor, (5) Desmos account (free).

Three Big Ideas in Geometry

1. Shapes Emerge from Construction Operations

A triangle isn't just a definition. It's the result of an operation: connecting three points with line segments. A circle isn't just a formula. It's the result of rotating a point around a center at a fixed distance.

Your task: Use Desmos Geometry to construct these shapes and discover their properties.

2. Transformations Reveal Hidden Relationships

When you rotate, reflect, or translate a shape, you discover something profound: some shapes are congruent (identical in size and shape), while others are similar (same shape, different size). These relationships aren't arbitrary—they reveal the order and beauty God built into creation.

Your task: Transform shapes and observe what stays the same and what changes.

3. Measurement Connects Shapes to Numbers

When you measure angles, distances, and areas, you discover universal patterns. The ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter is always . The sides of a right triangle always satisfy the Pythagorean theorem. These aren't coincidences—they're God's mathematical fingerprints in creation.

Your task: Measure shapes and discover the patterns hidden within them.

Your Exploration Task: Discovering Geometric Operations

Objective: Explore three fundamental geometric operations using Desmos Geometry and discover how shapes emerge from actions.

Exploration A: Building a Triangle

  1. Create three points (A, B, C) anywhere on the canvas
  2. Connect them with line segments to form a triangle
  3. Measure the three angles of your triangle
  4. Question: Add the three angles together. What do you notice? Try moving the vertices around. Does the sum change?
  5. Reflection: You just discovered the Triangle Angle Sum Theorem through operation. The operation (connecting three points) generated a shape with a hidden property (angles sum to 180°).

Exploration B: Building a Circle

  1. Create a point (center) and another point (on the circle)
  2. Use the circle tool to draw a circle with that center and radius
  3. Create several points on the circle
  4. Measure the distance from the center to each point on the circle
  5. Question: What do you notice about these distances? Why do you think this is true?
  6. Reflection: A circle is defined by an operation: rotating a point around a center at a fixed distance. All points on the circle are equidistant from the center.

Exploration C: Building a Right Angle

  1. Create two perpendicular lines (use the perpendicular line tool)
  2. Mark the point where they intersect
  3. Measure the angle between them
  4. Question: What is the measure of a right angle? Why do you think perpendicular lines create this specific angle?
  5. Reflection: Perpendicularity is an operation that creates a 90° angle. This operation is fundamental to geometry and appears everywhere in construction.
